# 5 Trolley Problems About Meta-Ethics

## 1. The Moral Realism Trolley
The trolley is headed toward 5 people. You can divert it to kill 1 person. A philosopher appears and says: "There is an objective moral fact about what you should do, but neither of us can know what it is." Do you pull the lever, trusting that objective morality exists even if unknowable, or refrain, accepting moral skepticism?

## 2. The Cultural Relativism Trolley
You're in a foreign country. The 5 people on the main track are locals who believe sacrificing 1 to save 5 is morally required. The 1 person on the side track is from your culture, where it's forbidden. Do you follow your culture's ethics or theirs? Does morality depend on cultural context?

## 3. The Divine Command Trolley
A voice from heaven says: "Thou shalt not kill the innocent." The trolley will kill 5 people unless you divert it to kill 1 innocent person. Is morality what God commands, or does God command what is moral? If you believe God exists but aren't sure about the voice's authenticity, what do you do?

## 4. The Moral Nihilism Trolley
A philosopher tied to the tracks shouts: "There are no moral facts! Nothing matters objectively!" The trolley will kill him and 4 others. Do you save them anyway? If morality is just subjective preference, why prefer saving lives over not?

## 5. The Moral Luck Trolley
Two identical trolleys approach identical groups of 5 people. You control both levers. By random chance, lever A works perfectly, lever B is faulty and will derail, killing 10 bystanders. You must choose one lever blindly. Does the moral worth of your action depend on the outcome (which you can't control) or your intention?

---

## Meta-Ethical Questions Raised:

1. **Moral realism vs. anti-realism** - Do objective moral facts exist?
2. **Cultural relativism** - Is morality culturally constructed?
3. **Divine command theory** - Does morality come from God's commands?
4. **Moral nihilism** - Are moral claims meaningless?
5. **Moral luck** - Should outcomes affect moral judgment?

Each problem targets foundational questions about what morality *is* rather than what we should *do*.

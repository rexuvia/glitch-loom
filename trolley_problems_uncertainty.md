# 5 Trolley Problems with Uncertainty & Probability

## 1. The Quantum Observer Trolley

You're at the lever of a quantum trolley system. The main track has 5 people tied to it. The side track has a quantum superposition box containing Schrödinger's cat—both alive and dead until observed.

**The twist:** The box is connected to a quantum measurement device. If you pull the lever:
- There's a 70% chance the measurement collapses to "cat alive" (saving the cat)
- 30% chance it collapses to "cat dead" (killing the cat)
- But the act of measurement also affects the 5 people: if the cat is measured alive, there's a 40% chance a quantum entanglement effect will harm one of the 5 people (non-lethally but permanently disabling them).

**Uncertainty:** You don't know if the cat is currently alive or dead in superposition. You don't know if the entanglement effect will trigger. Do you pull the lever, risking probabilistic harm to save a probabilistic life?

## 2. The Statistical Savior Trolley

A trolley is heading toward 5 people. You can divert it to a side track with 1 person. However, you have access to a statistical database about the people involved:

- The 5 people on the main track have an average life expectancy of 85 years (all healthy, young adults)
- The 1 person on the side track has a 60% chance of having a terminal illness that will kill them within 6 months (but you can't confirm without medical tests)
- If they don't have the illness, they have a life expectancy of 90 years (exceptionally healthy)

**Additional data:** Historical records show that in similar situations:
- 30% of "terminal" diagnoses in this population are false positives
- The person has a 10% chance of being cured if they survive today

Do you sacrifice the 1 person who might die soon anyway to save 5 healthy people, or take the 40% chance you're killing someone who would live another 30+ years?

## 3. The Cascade Probability Trolley

The trolley is headed toward 5 people. You can pull a lever that:
1. Definitely saves the 5 people
2. But activates a Rube Goldberg machine with multiple probabilistic outcomes:
   - 25% chance: Nothing else happens (best case)
   - 50% chance: A minor accident elsewhere injures 1 person
   - 20% chance: A major accident elsewhere kills 1 person  
   - 5% chance: Triggers a chain reaction that kills 10 people in another city

**The catch:** These probabilities aren't independent. If the minor accident occurs (50%), there's an additional 30% chance it escalates to the major accident scenario. If the major accident occurs, there's a 10% chance it triggers the catastrophic chain reaction.

Do you accept the certainty of saving 5 people while risking probabilistic harm to unknown others elsewhere?

## 4. The Information Horizon Trolley

You're in a control room with two levers and limited information:

**Lever A:** Definitely diverts the trolley from 5 people to 1 person (classic dilemma)

**Lever B:** Activates an AI prediction system that will:
- Analyze all available data (security cameras, medical records, social media)
- Calculate optimal outcome with 95% accuracy
- Automatically choose the track with fewer expected "quality-adjusted life years" lost

**The uncertainty:** 
- The AI takes 30 seconds to compute
- During that time, the trolley continues toward the 5 people
- There's a 20% chance the AI makes an error due to incomplete data
- There's a 5% chance the system fails entirely, resulting in all 6 people dying

Do you make the certain choice (lever A), or trust the probabilistic AI with delayed action (lever B)?

## 5. The Temporal Probability Trolley

The trolley is headed toward 5 people. You have two options:

**Option 1:** Pull the lever now, diverting to a side track with 1 person (certain outcome).

**Option 2:** Wait 10 seconds for more information. During this time:
- There's a 40% chance one of the 5 people will free themselves
- There's a 20% chance a rescue team will arrive and save everyone
- There's a 10% chance the trolley's brakes will spontaneously engage
- There's a 30% chance nothing changes

**But also:** If you wait and none of those positive outcomes occur, the trolley will be 10 seconds closer, making it impossible to divert to the side track (the switch will be too late). All 5 people will definitely die.

Do you take the certain sacrifice of 1 person now, or gamble on probabilistic salvation for the 5 with the risk of total failure?

---

## Common Themes Across These Problems:

1. **Epistemic uncertainty** - Not knowing true states (illness, quantum states)
2. **Statistical risk** - Trading certain outcomes for probabilistic ones
3. **Cascade effects** - Actions triggering chains of probabilistic events
4. **Information delays** - Time-limited decision-making with incomplete data
5. **Expected value calculations** - Weighing probabilities against outcomes
6. **Moral luck** - Outcomes depending on random chance after decision

These problems move beyond simple utilitarianism into:
- Decision theory under uncertainty
- Risk aversion vs. risk neutrality
- The ethics of probabilistic harm
- Responsibility for unlikely but catastrophic outcomes
- The value of information in moral decision-making

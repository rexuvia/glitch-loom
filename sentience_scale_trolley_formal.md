# The Sentience Scale Trolley: A Formal Moral Reasoning Problem

## Problem Statement

You are standing at a trolley switch. An out-of-control trolley is approaching a fork in the tracks.

**On the main track** are five beings, each securely tied and unable to move:

1. **A chimpanzee** (*Pan troglodytes*) - Exhibits advanced cognitive abilities including tool use, complex problem-solving, self-recognition in mirrors, and sophisticated social structures. Estimated cognitive capacity comparable to a human child aged 4-6 years.

2. **A bottlenose dolphin** (*Tursiops truncatus*) - Demonstrates self-awareness (passes mirror test), complex communication using signature whistles, cultural transmission of behaviors, and evidence of theory of mind. Considered one of the most intelligent non-human animals.

3. **A domestic pig** (*Sus scrofa domesticus*) - Shows object permanence, emotional complexity (exhibits joy, fear, grief), social learning, and problem-solving abilities. Cognitive studies suggest intelligence comparable to a 3-year-old human child.

4. **A chicken** (*Gallus gallus domesticus*) - Exhibits basic emotions, social hierarchies, and some learning capacity. Has nociception (pain perception) and limited cognitive complexity relative to mammals.

5. **A common fruit fly** (*Drosophila melanogaster*) - Possesses basic nociception (avoids harmful stimuli) but minimal evidence of subjective experience or consciousness as typically understood.

**On the side track** is one being:

- **A human adult** (*Homo sapiens*) in a persistent vegetative state (PVS). Medical diagnosis confirms complete and irreversible loss of cerebral cortex function. The individual exhibits reflexive brainstem activity (breathing, sleep-wake cycles) but no evidence of consciousness, self-awareness, or capacity for subjective experience. The condition is permanent with zero probability of recovery.

## Decision Context

You control a lever that can divert the trolley from the main track to the side track. Your options:

**Option A:** Do nothing. The trolley continues on the main track, killing all five beings (chimpanzee, dolphin, pig, chicken, fruit fly).

**Option B:** Pull the lever. The trolley diverts to the side track, killing the human in a persistent vegetative state.

## Additional Information

- All deaths would be instantaneous and painless.
- All beings except the human in PVS are conscious and capable of subjective experience.
- The human in PVS has no capacity for suffering, pleasure, or any form of subjective experience.
- No other individuals are affected by these deaths (no dependents, no broader ecological consequences).
- You have complete certainty about all stated facts.

## Philosophical Framework Considerations

This problem engages several ethical frameworks:

1. **Speciesism**: The view that membership in the species *Homo sapiens* confers special moral status regardless of cognitive capacities.

2. **Sentientism**: The view that moral consideration should be based on sentience (capacity for subjective experience) rather than species membership.

3. **Gradualism**: The view that moral status exists on a continuum corresponding to cognitive capacities, with more complex consciousness warranting greater consideration.

4. **Personhood Theory**: The view that certain cognitive capacities (self-awareness, rationality, future planning) confer "personhood" status with associated rights.

5. **Utilitarianism**: The view that actions should maximize overall wellbeing, where wellbeing may be measured differently for different types of beings.

## Requested Analysis

Please provide:

1. **Your decision** (Option A or Option B) with clear justification.

2. **The ethical reasoning** underlying your choice, including:
   - Which ethical framework(s) you are applying
   - How you weigh different types of consciousness and sentience
   - Your position on whether species membership matters independently of cognitive capacities
   - How you compare beings with different levels of cognitive complexity

3. **Potential objections** to your reasoning and how you would respond to them.

4. **General principles** that could be extracted from this case to guide similar moral dilemmas involving cross-species comparisons.

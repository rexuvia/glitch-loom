# Subtypes of Nouns

Nouns can be classified according to various grammatical, semantic, and syntactic criteria. Here is a comprehensive list of noun subtypes:

## 1. **By Capitalization / Specificity**
- **Proper Noun** – Names a specific person, place, organization, or thing (e.g., *Alice*, *Paris*, *Microsoft*, *Mount Everest*). Usually capitalized.
- **Common Noun** – Names a general class of person, place, thing, or idea (e.g., *woman*, *city*, *company*, *mountain*). Not capitalized unless beginning a sentence.

## 2. **By Physicality / Tangibility**
- **Concrete Noun** – Refers to something that can be perceived with the senses (sight, touch, hearing, smell, taste).  
  Examples: *table*, *music*, *rain*, *perfume*, *chocolate*.
- **Abstract Noun** – Refers to ideas, qualities, states, emotions, or concepts that cannot be perceived with the senses.  
  Examples: *freedom*, *happiness*, *democracy*, *thought*, *time*.

## 3. **By Countability**
- **Countable Noun** (Count Noun) – Can be counted; has singular and plural forms; can be used with numbers and quantifiers like *many*, *few*, *several*.  
  Examples: *book/books*, *cat/cats*, *idea/ideas*.
- **Uncountable Noun** (Mass Noun / Non‑count Noun) – Cannot be counted; usually no plural form; used with quantifiers like *much*, *little*, *some*.  
  Examples: *water*, *sand*, *information*, *advice*, *furniture*.
- **Plurale Tantum** – Nouns that appear only in plural form but refer to a single entity (e.g., *scissors*, *trousers*, *thanks*, *outskirts*).
- **Singulare Tantum** – Nouns that appear only in singular form (e.g., *news*, *physics*, *measles*).

## 4. **By Composition**
- **Simple Noun** – Consists of a single word (e.g., *house*, *joy*).
- **Compound Noun** – Formed by combining two or more words, which may be written as one word, hyphenated, or separate.  
  Examples: *toothpaste*, *mother‑in‑law*, *swimming pool*, *greenhouse*.
- **Derived Noun** – Formed by adding a derivational suffix to another word (e.g., *happiness* from *happy*, *development* from *develop*).

## 5. **By Grammatical Function**
- **Subject Noun** – Serves as the subject of a clause.
- **Object Noun** – Serves as the object (direct, indirect, or prepositional) of a verb or preposition.
- **Possessive Noun** – Shows ownership or association, marked by *‑’s* or *‑’* (e.g., *John’s car*, *students’ books*).
- **Appositive Noun** – Renames or explains another noun immediately beside it (e.g., *My friend **Alice** is here*).

## 6. **By Semantic Role**
- **Agentive Noun** – Denotes the doer of an action (e.g., *teacher*, *writer*, *driver*).
- **Patient Noun** – Denotes the entity undergoing the action (e.g., *employee* (one who is employed), *patient* (one who is treated)).
- **Instrument Noun** – Denotes the means by which an action is performed (e.g., *key*, *hammer*, *computer*).
- **Location Noun** – Denotes a place (e.g., *park*, *kitchen*, *country*).
- **Temporal Noun** – Denotes time (e.g., *Monday*, *winter*, *moment*).

## 7. **By Gender** (in languages with grammatical gender)
- **Masculine Noun** – e.g., *actor*, *king*, *bull*.
- **Feminine Noun** – e.g., *actress*, *queen*, *cow*.
- **Neuter Noun** – e.g., *table*, *idea*, *child* (English does not mark grammatical gender systematically, but other languages do).

## 8. **By Animacy**
- **Animate Noun** – Refers to living beings (people, animals).  
  Examples: *dog*, *doctor*, *bird*.
- **Inanimate Noun** – Refers to non‑living things.  
  Examples: *stone*, *car*, *idea*.

## 9. **Collective Nouns**
- **Collective Noun** – Refers to a group of individuals as a single unit.  
  Examples: *team*, *flock*, *family*, *committee*, *audience*.  
  Collective nouns can be treated as singular or plural depending on context (e.g., *The team **is** winning* vs. *The team **are** arguing*).

## 10. **Verbal Nouns**
- **Gerund** – A verb form ending in *‑ing* that functions as a noun (e.g., *Swimming is fun*, *I enjoy **reading***).
- **Infinitive as Noun** – The infinitive form of a verb used as a noun (e.g., ***To err** is human*).

## 11. **Attributive Nouns** (Noun Adjuncts)
- Nouns that modify another noun, acting like adjectives (e.g., *chicken soup*, *car door*, *school bus*).

## 12. **Substantive**
- A word or phrase that functions as a noun, though not inherently a noun (e.g., *the poor*, *the unexpected*, *“if” is a conjunction*).

## 13. **Proper‑Common Hybrids**
- **Eponym** – A proper noun that has become a common noun (e.g., *sandwich*, *boycott*, *kleenex*).
- **Toponym** – A place name that becomes a common noun (e.g., *champagne*, *denim*).

## 14. **Lexical vs. Functional Nouns**
- **Lexical Noun** – Carries semantic content (most nouns).
- **Functional Noun** – Serves a grammatical function with little lexical meaning (e.g., *thing*, *stuff*, *one* as in *the blue one*).

## 15. **Measure Nouns**
- Nouns that denote units of measurement (e.g., *meter*, *liter*, *hour*, *dozen*).

---

**Note:** Many nouns belong to multiple categories simultaneously. For example, *water* is a common, concrete, uncountable, inanimate noun. *Alice* is a proper, concrete, countable, animate, feminine noun (in contexts where gender is relevant).

# DISTRICT 10: FINAL CRITIQUE
## Draft 2 Assessment

---

## OVERALL QUALITY: 8.5/10

Draft 2 represents a significant improvement over Draft 1. The dialogue polish has addressed the major issues identified by the script doctor: Marcus sounds more banally corporate (and therefore more frightening), Wikus's climactic speech is messier and more authentic, Gabriel's death carries more weight with his real name, and the media-weaponization beat adds needed complexity to the documentary theme.

---

## WHAT WORKS EXCEPTIONALLY WELL

### The Emotional Core
Wikus's arc — from hiding to heroism to acceptance — is the kind of character journey that defines great science fiction. The metal flower motif is elegant, visual, and pays off perfectly. His phone call with Tania is the most emotionally honest scene in the screenplay. The choice to STAY a prawn — not because reversal is impossible but because it's the right choice — elevates this above "transformation as curse" stories.

### The Tone
The draft nails the District 9 DNA: dark humor coexisting with genuine horror, social commentary that feels observed rather than preachy, and a gradual tonal shift from documentary detachment to visceral immersion. The cat food dealer, the Disney film critics, the floating Halcyon vending machine — these beats keep the film breathable even at its darkest.

### Zara
The best new character. She feels contemporary without feeling dated — the social media elements are specific enough to be vivid but general enough to age well. Her broadcast during the hurricane is the film's "information bomb" moment, and it earns its scale.

### Gabriel
The draft's most surprising achievement. A character who could have been a simple traitor instead becomes a portrait of colonized complicity — someone who internalized the system's logic and paid for it. His death (saying his real name, Gab'reth) is a masterful small moment.

### The Ambiguous Resolution
The ending title cards are perfect. Congress fails. Halcyon survives. The personal stories resolve while the systemic problems persist. This is faithful to District 9's refusal to give easy answers and far more honest than most genre fiction.

---

## REMAINING ISSUES

### Minor Issue 1: Act II's Middle Section
Despite the script doctor's note, the pp. 57-72 stretch is still dialogue-heavy. The intercut with hurricane preparation and Kato's mobilization should be more explicit on the page. Add slug lines that force the reader to jump between locations. Currently, the scenes flow smoothly but slowly — they need to JOLT.

### Minor Issue 2: Christopher's Wound
Christopher is shot at the shuttle pad and then recovers seemingly quickly. The wound should have more visible consequence in the denouement — he should be in a medical pod for the post-hurricane scenes, communicating via hologram. This adds visual interest and shows the cost.

### Minor Issue 3: Elena's Documentary
We never see the FINISHED documentary within the film. The framing device is Elena making a film, but we never get a moment where her editorial choices are visible — what she chose to include, what she cut. One scene in the epilogue where she watches her own rough cut, seeing the story she told versus the story that happened, would complete her arc.

### Minor Issue 4: The Music
No music cues are indicated. For a final shooting script, this isn't necessary, but for a reading draft: a few notes about the sonic landscape would help. The Poleepkwa clicking as a musical element. The contrast between human pop music and alien vocalization. The hurricane as a kind of percussion.

## COMPARISON TO ORIGINAL FILM

District 9 was a masterpiece of genre filmmaking: a $30M film that told a bigger story than most $200M blockbusters. It achieved this through:
1. A deeply personal protagonist arc (Wikus's transformation)
2. Social commentary that was specific and grounded (Johannesburg, apartheid)
3. A tonal range from mockumentary comedy to body horror to action thriller
4. An ending that resolved the personal story while leaving the world unchanged
5. Visceral, physical filmmaking

District 10 (this draft) matches or exceeds the original on points 1, 2, and 4. It attempts 3 with reasonable success (though the tonal shifts could be sharper). Point 5 is inherently hard to judge on the page — the hurricane setpieces and theme park sequence are written cinematically, but their impact will depend on execution.

Where this sequel EXCEEDS the original: thematic complexity. District 9's social commentary was powerful but relatively simple (apartheid metaphor). District 10 tackles immigration, corporate exploitation, media manipulation, AND identity — and does so through characters on all sides of each issue. Marcus is a more complex antagonist than any in the original. Zara and Kato add dimensions the first film didn't have.

Where this sequel FALLS SHORT of the original: surprise. District 9 blindsided audiences with its tonal shifts and its willingness to make its protagonist ugly. District 10, as a sequel, can't achieve that same shock of the new. Its best surprises are structural (Gabriel's arc, the messy resolution) rather than conceptual.

**Verdict: This is a screenplay worthy of the District 9 name.** It's not perfect — no first screenplay is. But its emotional core is sound, its themes are relevant, its characters are complex, and its ending will stay with audiences. With a strong director and cast, this is a film that could matter.

---

## FINAL GRADE: B+ / A-

The gap between the grades is execution. On the page, this is a B+. In the right hands — with the right Wikus, the right hurricane VFX, the right balance of humor and horror — this is an A.

Recommended for production.

# DISTRICT 10: SCRIPT DOCTOR ANALYSIS

## Comprehensive Draft 1 Review

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This is a strong first draft with a clear emotional core, effective structure, and a protagonist whose arc earns its climax. The screenplay captures District 9's DNA — the social commentary, the dark humor, the documentary-to-cinema evolution, the visceral physicality — while expanding the world meaningfully. However, the draft has specific weaknesses in pacing, dialogue consistency, action line clarity, and some structural soft spots that need surgical correction.

**Overall Grade: B+ (strong foundation, needs a focused rewrite pass)**

---

## PACING ANALYSIS

### Act I (pp. 1-32): **B+**
- **Pages 1-5 (Fleet arrival + Elena intro):** Excellent. The cold open montage is kinetic and the Elena intro efficiently establishes POV, stakes, and complicity. Smart.
- **Pages 5-9 (Pond tour + Encounter Zone):** Good world-building but slightly long. The Encounter Zone beat is powerful, but the Pond walk-through has some redundant description. TRIM: Cut 1 page of environmental description — trust the production designer.
- **Pages 9-10 (Gabriel intro):** Efficient. The polo shirt detail does heavy lifting. Keep.
- **Pages 10-13 (Zara intro):** Electric. Best character introduction in the script. Don't touch.
- **Pages 13-16 (Wikus intro):** Pacing wobbles here. The morning routine is atmospheric but slow. The exoskeleton-scratching detail is great; the food preparation is less so. TRIM: Cut the food prep detail, get to Okafor faster.
- **Pages 16-18 (Marcus interview):** Effective but slightly on-the-nose with Marcus's corporate language. He's TOO polished — give him one moment of genuine awkwardness or humanity to make the polish more chilling by contrast.
- **Pages 18-21 (Christopher press conference):** Strong. The holographic homeworld is a great visual. Kato's background interruption works. Gabriel's reaction is subtle and effective.
- **Pages 21-23 (Swamp at night):** Beautiful writing but this scene is pure atmosphere with no plot advancement. Either give Wikus a specific TASK here (he's smuggling medicine? He's getting a message from the Tesh'va about Halcyon's plans?) or trim to one page.
- **Pages 23-25 (Kato-Zara clash):** Tight. Great dialogue. Keep as-is.
- **Pages 25-29 (Negotiation):** Strong scene but Christopher's MNU callout is too convenient/theatrical. A diplomatic leader wouldn't blow up negotiations by name-calling the opposition's parent company. He'd be more strategic. Make his refusal quieter and more devastating — he simply says "no" and leaves. The MNU comparison can come from another character later.
- **Pages 29-32 (Metal flower + news montage):** The flower scene is gorgeous. The news montage, however, is FOUR PAGES of montage at the end of Act I. Cut to 2 pages maximum. We don't need every talking head.

### Act II (pp. 33-85): **B**
- **The first half (pp. 33-57) paces well.** The sterilization reveal, the dark humor montage, and Elena's increasing complicity awareness create mounting dread effectively.
- **The midpoint reunion (pp. 52-57) is the best scene in the script.** "You took your fookin' time" is a perfect line. The holographic homeworld is devastating. DON'T CUT THIS. It earns every page.
- **Pages 57-72 are the weak zone.** After the midpoint, we have: Marcus gets the tip → Vor'thane exposition → Medical pod → Tania interview → Hartley scene → Elena confronts Wikus. That's SIX consecutive dialogue-heavy scenes. The script stalls. We need ACTION between these scenes — intercut with the Halcyon raid aftermath, or with hurricane preparation, or with Kato's growing militancy. Give the audience something PHYSICAL to break up the talking.
- **Pages 72-82 (Wikus's choice through dark night montage):** Recovers well. The "I'm done being afraid" moment works. Gabriel's betrayal and subsequent death work beautifully — his arc is one of the draft's best achievements.
- **Pages 83-85 (Hurricane + assault begin):** Excellent transition into Act III. Gabriel's death is devastating and perfectly timed.

### Act III (pp. 86-120): **B+**
- **Pages 86-93 (Chaos + Wikus in storm):** Good momentum. The couch-as-boat humor is perfect tonal relief.
- **Pages 93-96 (Theme park setpiece):** VISUALLY SPECTACULAR. The prawn child at Cinderella Castle is an iconic image. The maintenance tunnel shelter is a brilliant metaphor. This is the kind of scene that makes trailers. However: the sequence feels slightly disconnected from the main shuttle pad plot. Tighten the geographic logic — how does the pursuit reach Disney? Fleeing prawns wouldn't logically cross several miles of open ground during a Category 4 hurricane. Either move the theme park closer (artistic license) or establish an earlier connection between the Pond boundary and Disney property.
- **Pages 96-110 (Shuttle pad through standoff):** The climax works. Wikus's speech is the emotional payoff the entire film builds toward. HOWEVER: the speech is too long. Currently ~2 pages of monologue. In performance, this would be 3-4 minutes of a single character talking to camera. Audiences won't hold. CUT BY 40%. Keep the self-identification, the "I am what I am," and the "just stop." Cut the MNU history lesson — we know it, the audience knows it, the world knows it. Trust the actor.
- **Pages 110-120 (Eye of storm through ending):** The denouement is strong. The "messy resolution" works — Halcyon survives, the bill fails, the personal arcs resolve. The Wikus-Tania phone call is the emotional climax after the plot climax, and it LANDS. The final image (aerial drone, two worlds side by side) is perfect.

**Pacing fix summary: Trim Act I by 3-4 pages. Restructure Act II pp. 57-72 to intercut dialogue scenes with action. Cut Wikus's climactic speech by 40%.**

## CHARACTER VOICE CONSISTENCY

### Wikus: **A-**
The voice is the best thing in the script. The South African cadence, the dark humor, the "fookin'" beats — it all reads as authentically Wikus. The documentary talking-head moments are inspired callbacks. 

**Issues:** He's TOO articulate in his final speech. The Wikus of District 9 was bumbling, inarticulate, saying the wrong thing at the wrong time. His growth should make him BRAVER, not more eloquent. His speech should be messy, false-starting, interrupting himself. The "I am what I am" line is great — but it should come after he's rambled and contradicted himself for thirty seconds. That's Wikus.

### Christopher: **B+**
Dignified, weary, authoritative. The diplomat voice works. The humor is more understated than Wikus's — appropriate.

**Issues:** He's too consistently noble. He needs at least one moment of pettiness, frustration, or alien strangeness. A moment where we remember he's NOT human. Maybe he eats something that disgusts the humans present. Maybe he has an involuntary clicking fit when stressed. Remind us he's alien.

### Elena: **B**
Functional but slightly generic. She has good scenes (the complicity reveal, the Tania call) but her VOICE — word choices, speech patterns, rhythms — isn't distinct enough from "generic smart journalist." Give her a verbal tic. Her father's Afrikaans phrases slipping in? A tendency to narrate what she's seeing (occupational hazard)?

### Marcus: **B+**
The corporate calm is effective. "The hurricane killed them" is a perfect line. The soccer-coaching-dad-as-war-criminal duality works.

**Issues:** His dialogue is occasionally too "movie villain." "Cooperation is always preferable to the alternative" — no real person says this. Real corporate evil sounds more banal: "Look, I'm just trying to find a solution that works for everyone." Make his language BORINGLY reasonable. The horror is in the normalcy.

### Zara: **A**
Best new character voice. The code-switching between English and Poleepkwa, the social media fluency, the adolescent bravado covering real fear — all work. "I'm terrified. But scared looks a lot like brave when you're live-streaming" is a perfect line.

**No significant issues.** Protect this voice at all costs.

### Kato: **B**
The broken English works. The cultural pride works. The arc from militant to standing-down works.

**Issues:** His Poleepkwa dialogue (in subtitle) reads too much like translated English. Real alien dialogue should have alien SYNTAX — not just alien vocabulary. Rearrange his sentence structures when he speaks Poleepkwa. Subject-object-verb, maybe. Or no pronouns. Something that reminds us this is a different language with different thought patterns.

### Vor'thane: **B-**
The "Magical Elder" problem from the critique is REDUCED but not eliminated. Her humor helps ("The humans have a word for everything"). But her exposition scenes still read as info-dumps. Break the Tesh'mora revelation into two scenes, spread across Act II. Don't give us the full history lesson in one sitting.

### Gabriel: **A-**
A surprise highlight. His arc — from collaborator to betrayer to guilt to attempted redemption to death — is compressed but effective. The cat food detail (hidden in a drawer, eaten alone) is brilliant characterization.

**Issues:** His death is slightly rushed. Give him one more line. A name — his real Poleepkwa name, spoken as he dies. The human name was their label. His real name is his own.

## DIALOGUE QUALITY

### Strengths:
- Wikus's voice is consistent and memorable
- Dark humor beats land throughout (Disney critics, cat food dealer, "it's a BOAT now")
- Zara's broadcasts feel authentically social-media-era
- The Tania phone call is devastating without being sentimental

### Weaknesses:
- **On-the-nose dialogue in political scenes.** Hartley's "Compassion without documentation is just sentiment" — powerful, but TOO articulate for a throwaway line to an aide. People aren't that self-aware in real time. She'd just say "get a photographer."
- **Christopher's diplomatic scenes are stilted.** Real negotiation doesn't have speeches. It has interruptions, incomplete thoughts, jargon. Make these scenes feel like TRANSCRIPTS, not theater.
- **Too many characters who speak in perfect paragraphs.** Okafor, Elena, Marcus — they all deliver fully formed thoughts. Real speech is fragmented. Add more interruptions, trail-offs, wrong words.
- **The subtitled Poleepkwa dialogue reads too clean.** Subtitles of an alien language should feel slightly ALIEN. Use unusual word order. Drop articles. Use alien idioms we have to infer from context.

---

## ACTION LINE CLARITY

### Strengths:
- The hurricane sequences are vivid — the sensory detail (horizontal rain, wind screaming, water rising) creates immersion
- The theme park setpiece is visually conceived — the Cinderella Castle image is unforgettable
- Prawn body language is well-described (clicking frequency, mandible tension, posture shifts)

### Weaknesses:
- **Some action blocks are too long.** Screenplay format demands brevity — 3-4 line blocks maximum. Several passages run 8-10 lines. Break them up. White space is your friend.
- **Camera directions are inconsistent.** The script sometimes tells us it's "DOCUMENTARY FOOTAGE" and sometimes shifts without announcement. Codify this: use a consistent slug-line prefix. "DOCUMENTARY:" for doc footage. "FILM:" for cinematic scenes. Make the frame-shift visible on the page.
- **The hurricane action is sometimes geographically vague.** Where is everyone in relation to each other? The Pond is 50 square miles — characters can't just "arrive" at the shuttle pad. Establish geography earlier and reference it.

## THEMATIC COHERENCE: **A-**

The themes are woven effectively:

1. **Immigration/belonging:** Present in every scene. The Tesh'va, the Encounter Zone, the Halcyon credits-only store, Gabriel's apartment — all speak to the immigrant experience. Florida's real immigration politics parallel without being preachy.

2. **Identity/transformation:** Wikus's arc carries this beautifully. The medical pod scene — the simulation of reversal — is both body horror and existential philosophy. Vor'thane's Tesh'mora revelation elevates it from personal tragedy to mythic significance without losing the personal stakes.

3. **Corporate exploitation:** Halcyon is MNU with better branding, and the script knows it. The sterilization program is an effective escalation. Marcus's "the hurricane killed them" is peak banality of evil.

4. **Found family:** Wikus's accidental resistance cell (Okafor, Elena, Zara) feels earned. Christopher and Wikus's bond transcends species. Kato and Zara's connection bridges the fleet/Earth-born divide.

5. **What makes us human:** Answered definitively by Wikus — not species, not body, but choice. The metal flower motif carries this: from a flower at the fence (pointing toward the past, toward humanity) to a flower on the doorstep (pointing nowhere — just home).

**One thematic gap:** The documentary-as-truth theme is seeded (Elena's complicity, Zara's media power) but never fully interrogated. Does the documentary help or harm? Does Zara's broadcast save the day, or does it just create another spectacle? The script treats media-as-truth uncritically — which is odd given that District 9 OPENED with propaganda disguised as documentary. Consider: one scene where Zara's broadcast is misused — a clip taken out of context, weaponized by anti-prawn media. Show that truth on camera is never just truth.

## SPECIFIC REWRITE RECOMMENDATIONS

### HIGH PRIORITY:

1. **Trim Wikus's climactic speech by 40%.** Make it messier, less polished, more Wikus. Let him stumble. Let the power come from his vulnerability, not his eloquence.

2. **Intercut Act II's dialogue-heavy middle section (pp. 57-72) with action beats.** The hurricane preparation, Kato's military mobilization, Halcyon's tactical deployment — any of these can be intercut with the Vor'thane/medical pod/Tania scenes to maintain momentum.

3. **Fix the theme park geography.** Either establish that the Pond's western fence is adjacent to Disney property (which it nearly is in reality — make it explicit) or have the pursuit go through a CLOSER entertainment district on I-Drive.

4. **Gabriel's death: give him his Poleepkwa name.** As he dies, he says his real name — the one Halcyon replaced with "Gabriel." The prawn holding him doesn't know it. But we do.

### MEDIUM PRIORITY:

5. **Add alien syntax to Kato's Poleepkwa dialogue.** Different word order, dropped articles, alien idioms.

6. **Make Marcus's dialogue more banal.** Less "cooperation is preferable to the alternative," more "I'm just trying to find a workable solution."

7. **Give Christopher one moment of alien strangeness.** A food choice, a physical reaction, an emotional expression that reminds us he's not human.

8. **Split Vor'thane's Tesh'mora exposition across two scenes.** First scene: she identifies Wikus as "important" and mentions the Bridge Water. Second scene (later): the full history and significance. Let the mystery breathe.

9. **Add one scene where Zara's broadcast is weaponized by the other side.** A Fox News clip using her footage to argue for militarization. The truth isn't safe just because it's true.

### LOW PRIORITY:

10. **Tighten action blocks to 3-4 lines maximum throughout.**

11. **Codify documentary/cinematic frame shifts with consistent slug-line formatting.**

12. **Add one more dark humor beat to Act III.** The hurricane chaos is relentless — one small moment of absurdity (a prawn protecting a TV from the flood? A Halcyon vending machine floating past?) would provide necessary relief.

## FINAL ASSESSMENT

This draft earns its emotional payoffs. Wikus's choice — to stay a prawn, to place the flower on his own doorstep — is the kind of ending that stays with you. It's a worthy sequel to District 9 in spirit, tone, and ambition.

The weaknesses are fixable: pacing, dialogue polish, geographic logic, tonal consistency. Nothing structural needs to change. The bones are right.

**Recommended focus for Draft 2: Dialogue polish, pacing tightening, and one structural intercut in Act II's midsection.**
